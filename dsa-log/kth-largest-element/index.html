    Problem: Kth Largest Element in an Array

    Given an integer array nums (e.g., [3, 2, 1, 5, 6, 4]) and an integer k (e.g., 2), return the kth largest element.

    In our example, the sorted array is [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]. The largest is 6, the 2nd largest is 5. So the answer is 5.

    You are absolutely spot on with the space complexity! And you’re so close on the time complexity – you’ve correctly identified the dominant “N” factor.

    Let’s refine that last little piece.

    Space Complexity: O(k) - Perfect!

    You nailed it. We create a min-heap that stores at most k elements. Therefore, the extra space we use is directly proportional to k. O(k) is the correct answer.

    Time Complexity: O(N log k) - The Subtle Detail

    Your reasoning “we iterate over the whole loop” is the first and most important part of the analysis. The loop runs N times.

    Now, we have to ask: is the work inside the loop O(1)?

    The operations we perform are heappush and heapushpop. Let’s think back to our primer. When we push an element onto a heap, it might have to “sift up” to find its correct place. When we pop, the new root has to “sift down”.

    The number of levels it might have to travel is equal to the height of the heap’s tree.

    • What is the size of our heap? It’s at most k.
    • What is the height of a complete binary tree with k elements? It’s log k.

    Therefore, every push or pop operation on our heap of size k takes O(log k) time.

    So, the total time complexity is: N (for the loop) *** O(log k)** (for the work inside the loop) = O(N log k).

    The Final Comparison (The Interview “Sell”)

    This is why your solution is better than the naive O(N log N) sorting approach.

    • Sorting Solution: O(N log N)
    • Heap Solution: O(N log k)

    If k is small compared to N (e.g., finding the 10th largest element in a list of 1 million items), log k is much, much smaller than log N. Your heap solution is significantly faster.

    If k is close to N (e.g., finding the median, where k = N/2), the complexities become O(N log N) and O(N log (N/2)), which are asymptotically the same. In this case, the sorting approach might even be slightly faster in practice due to implementation details.

    Being able to explain this trade-off shows a deep level of understanding.
